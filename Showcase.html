Final Project  ·  A Curated Selection

Spin, Turn
& Rotate

Eight projects where perception becomes an act of participation — records of the earth, wheels of light, choruses of seashells, gardens that answer your voice, singing benches, a voice that falls down a spiral, breath made visible, and a globe you spin to tune the world. 01

Yuri Suzuki  ·  2012  ·  Spherical record

The Sound of the Earth

“A spherical record whose grooves trace the outlines of the continents.”

Each country on the globe is engraved with a different sound. As the needle crosses it, field recordings gathered over four years play out — national anthems, traditional folk music, popular song and spoken-word broadcasts.

An aural journey around the world in thirty minutes.

Photo — Hitomi Kai Yoda  ·  SFMOMA Collection

02

Daily tous les jours  ·  Laval, CA

Coloriage

“Spin the wheel and colour the room with light.”

Built for the Jean-Piaget school — where some 120 students with physical and intellectual disabilities turn a single spinning gesture into a digital animation across an illuminated LED panel.

High-contrast, low-resolution light keeps the play legible to limited vision. Part start-of-day ritual, part meditative game, part simple creative expression.

Creative direction — Mouna Andraos & Melissa Mongiat

03

Moritz Simon Geist  ·  2021–2025  ·  Seashell, motor, aluminium

Hard Times — Soft Sounds

“Twenty seashells, filled with water and turned slowly — each its own acoustic instrument.”

No loudspeakers, no microphones. Rotating shells set air bubbles in motion, producing fragile, ever-changing ASMR-like tones that invite deep, close listening.

Made during the pandemic as a meditation on slowness, repetition and ecology; videos of the shells have reached more than fifty million viewers.

Photo — Pinelopi Gerasimou for Onassis Stegi

04

Yuri Suzuki  ·  2013  ·  V&A, London Design Festival

Garden of Russolo

“Speak into the garden and hear your voice returned — twisted and amplified.”

A grove of phonograph-like wooden boxes re-interprets the visitor's own voice into a muffled, atmospheric output, twisting and amplifying the original sound.

Named for the Futurist painter and composer Luigi Russolo, one of the first to treat noise itself as music.

Commissioned by the V&A  ·  Coventry University Collection

05

panGenerator  ·  2018  ·  Szczecin Philharmonic

Spiralalala

“Sing into a ball, then watch your voice fall thirty-five metres down a spiral.”

The Philharmonic's iconic spiral staircase becomes a 35-metre, voice-transforming instrument. Speak into a ball, release it, and infrared gates track its descent — bending your recording in time with the ball's speed and position.

Five speakers, one per floor, let the sound fall with the ball, while a light trail and projection mapping chase it down.

panGenerator — Cybulski · Goliński · Koźniewski

06

Daily tous les jours  ·  2022  ·  South Bend, US

Daydreamer

“Rock the bench and a chorus of human voices answers — soprano, alto, tenor.”

A series of slowly rocking, spinning benches that compose gentle music together. Every sound is a recording of the human voice; the notes depend on each bench's position and direction, the volume on the speed of its motion.

Move two benches in sync and the voices sing more intricate harmonies — turning a moment of pause into a shared, therapeutic ritual.

Photo — Leah Tribbett  ·  Creative direction Mouna Andraos & Melissa Mongiat

07

Florian Dussopt Design Studio  ·  2013  ·  London Design Festival

Blow

“Blow on the pinwheel and light scatters across the disk.”

A large, dark matte disk embedded with LEDs, a folded paper pinwheel at its centre. Blow on the pinwheel and a turbine reads how fast it spins, lighting the LEDs in proportion — dispersing light in an elegant array.

Spin it hard enough and the disk stays lit until the next breath, rewarding a small, intimate gesture.

Florian Dussopt Design Studio  ·  Dev. assistant Jonathan Joanes  ·  Photo Bart Pajak

08

Jude Pullen  ·  2020  ·  DesignSpark · Raspberry Pi

RadioGlobe

“Spin the globe, drop the reticle on a city, and its radio comes on.”

A desk globe rigged with two 1024-position rotary encoders — one at the South Pole reading longitude, one at the side reading latitude — wired to a Raspberry Pi. Turn it under the pointer and it streams from over 2,000 internet stations worldwide.

Built open-source in a 2×2-metre shed during the 2020 lockdown, to put the whole world's music, news and chatter a single spin away.

Jude Pullen  ·  Software Donald Robson  ·  Commissioned by DesignSpark
